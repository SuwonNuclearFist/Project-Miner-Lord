DSMAS

Dynamic Sequential Market Adjustment Simulation

The Dynamic Sequential Market Adjustment Simulation (DSMAS) is the economic and social simulation layer of Project Miner Lord.

It is designed to explain how a province actually lives through production, supply, acquisition, use, storage, shortage, seasonal pressure, and long-run social reproduction.

The purpose of DSMAS is not to create a perfectly balanced abstract economy.

Its purpose is to create a world in which economic life feels alive, constrained, and visible to the player.

What DSMAS Is

DSMAS is a sequential simulation.

This means that the world changes step by step rather than all at once.

Goods are produced first, then supplied, then acquired, then used, and then carried over into later periods if they remain in stock.

At the same time, estates make medium-run decisions about reserve building, fertility, child investment, and productive investment.

Over a much longer horizon, those choices affect the next generation, estate mobility, and the long-run development of a province.

In short, DSMAS connects three different layers of change:

  • short-run material flow
  • medium-run estate planning
  • long-run social transformation

What DSMAS Is Not

DSMAS is not built around one instant, perfect, fully synchronized economic balance.

It does not assume that if a good exists somewhere in the realm, everyone who needs it can automatically use it.

It does not assume that goods instantly move to the right place.

It does not assume that survival pressure, social reproduction, and productive growth all happen at the same speed.

Instead, DSMAS is built around delay, access, storage, transport, and uneven adjustment.

That is why stock, shortage, and seasonal pressure are so important.

Why DSMAS Exists

The main reason for DSMAS is to support a kind of gameplay where the player feels like a ruler of provinces rather than an accountant of isolated households.

The player should not have to manually optimize every tiny transaction.

At the same time, the player should still feel that:

  • reserve planning matters
  • winter preparation matters
  • route access matters
  • local shortage matters
  • administration matters
  • the next generation matters

This is why the system is designed around province-level management and estate-level aggregation.

Core Design Idea

The core idea of DSMAS is simple:

  • people and estates react to what recently happened
  • production does not automatically guarantee usable access
  • stock can protect a province, but only up to a point
  • climate and season change what people need
  • long-run social change happens through child development and cohort replacement
  • politics shapes the constraints under which all of this happens

This means the system is not mainly about finding one perfect price or one perfect formula.

It is about understanding how different pressures accumulate and interact over time.

Why Sequential Adjustment Matters

Sequential adjustment is central because it makes the world readable.

If everything were solved all at once, many of the most important gameplay experiences would disappear.

For example:

  • a province could not visibly fail to prepare for winter
  • storage decisions would matter less
  • transport bottlenecks would be weaker
  • local reserve rules would be less meaningful
  • politics would have fewer visible channels through which to distort or improve outcomes

By contrast, when the world adjusts in sequence, the player can see where the problem came from.

A problem may come from:

  • weak production
  • poor supply allocation
  • failed delivery
  • limited stock access
  • weak reserve policy
  • long-run developmental weakness

This is important both for immersion and for player learning.

Why Stock Management Is Central

Stock management is one of the core player experiences in DSMAS.

This is intentional.

A province should not feel safe merely because it produced well once.

It should feel safe because it has enough accessible reserve to survive later pressure.

This is especially important under:

  • winter demand spike
  • harvest seasonality
  • route disruption
  • war or insecurity
  • local administrative failure

In DSMAS, stock matters because time is uneven.

Some goods are produced only in certain periods.

Some goods are needed more urgently in certain seasons.

Some provinces are harder to reach than others.

That is why reserve building, minimum stock rules, and storage priorities are not secondary features.

They are part of the core simulation.

Why Stock Does Not Solve Everything

Although stock is central, it is not meant to solve every problem automatically.

If stock were unlimited and universally accessible, many interesting pressures would disappear.

For this reason, DSMAS relies on several constraints:

  • Storage Capacity Constraint
  • Access Constraint
  • Working-Capital Lock-Up
  • Seasonal Demand Spike
  • Storage Priority Rule
  • Minimum Stock Rule

These make stock powerful but not all-powerful.

A province may have goods in storage and still suffer stress if:

  • the reserve is too small
  • the reserve is hard to access
  • too much working capital is tied up
  • winter demand rises sharply
  • distribution within the province is unequal
  • politics or administration blocks actual use

Why Seasonality and Climate Matter

Seasonality is not just decoration in DSMAS.

It changes both production and need.

Agriculture is seasonal.

Fuel demand is seasonal.

Housing pressure is seasonal.

Basic survival requirement can also vary by climate and time of year.

This is why DSMAS treats Climate Zone and Seasonal State as meaningful background conditions.

A colder province should not feel identical to a warmer one.

A province approaching winter should not make the same decisions as a province approaching harvest.

This makes the world more legible, more strategic, and more grounded.

Why Acquisition and Use Are Separate

One important design choice in DSMAS is the separation between Acquisition and Use.

This means that buying or obtaining a good is not the same thing as consuming it immediately.

This matters because people often need to acquire goods before they need to use them.

Examples include:

  • grain after harvest
  • fuel before winter
  • reserve clothing for cold seasons
  • strategic reserve for local instability

This distinction helps the system represent stockpiling, winter preparation, and delayed survival pressure in a more natural way.

Why Next-Generation Human Capital Matters

Long-run social change in DSMAS is driven mainly through the next generation.

The system does not focus mainly on adult self-improvement.

Instead, it focuses on:

  • child support
  • child education
  • nutrition and stability
  • institutional access
  • cohort replacement

This approach fits the intended historical atmosphere better and also makes political and social reform more meaningful.

A realm is changed not only by surviving the next shortage, but by shaping what kind of adults the next generation becomes.

How DSMAS Connects to Politics

DSMAS is not isolated from the Politics system.

Politics determines the higher-order conditions under which economic life unfolds.

Politics affects things such as:

  • who has authority
  • who can enforce rules
  • who can access reserve
  • how strongly routes are protected
  • whether local administration is effective
  • how institutions shape mobility and opportunity

In this sense:

  • Politics defines the constraint structure
  • DSMAS simulates what happens inside that structure over time

Summary

The main purpose of DSMAS is to create an economy that is:

  • understandable
  • visible
  • strategically meaningful
  • low in tedious micromanagement
  • rich in long-run consequence

It is not meant to feel like a hidden spreadsheet.

It is meant to feel like a living provincial world in which production, stock, season, access, and social reproduction all matter in ways the player can actually see.
